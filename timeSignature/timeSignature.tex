Recall that notes are organized into \textbf{measures}, or \textbf{bars}, and that all the measures in a music piece have the same length unless otherwise stated. The \textbf{time signature} tells us how much music fits in each measure.
\begin{center}
 \begin{tikzpicture}
  \node at (0,0) {\meterfrac{\raisebox{.2em}{M}}{W}};
  \node[anchor=west] at (1,1) {number of beats in a measure};
  \node[anchor=west] at (1,-1) {number of beats in a whole note};
  \draw[->] (1,.9) -- (.4,.4);
  \draw[->] (1,-.9) -- (.4,-.4);
 \end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

  Notice how this again is similar to how we read fractions. In a fraction, the denominator (the number on the bottom) tells us in how many equal parts we break a whole, while the numerator (the number above) tells how many parts we take.
  
  With time signature, the number on the bottom tells us in how many beats we will break the whole into, while the number above tells us how many of those parts or beats we will use for each measure.
  
  For example in the passage below, from Petzold's \textit{Minuet in G Major}, the time signature is
  $\timesig{3}{4}$:
  \begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=.8\textwidth]{menuett34.png}
  \end{center}
  Here a whole note is broken into $4$ beats, and each measure has $3$ beats, meaning there are $3$ quarter notes per measure.  \textbf{Verify in the staff above that each measure adds to $\sfrac{3}{4}$ notes.} 
  
  However, the value of the time signature is not purely quantitative, it has a qualitative side as well. A time signature of \timesig{3}{4} means one should ``count''
  \begin{center}
  ONE-two-three  ONE-two-three  ONE-two-three
  \end{center}
 stressing the first note of every measure. Here is how some other time signatures are counted:
\begin{itemize}
\item In \timesig{4}{4} time, we count:  ONE-two-three-four
\item In \timesig{6}{8} time, we count: ONE-two-three-FOUR-five-six 
\item In \timesig{5}{4} time, we count:  ONE-two-three-ONE-two 
\end{itemize}

Different time signatures naturally emphasize different aspects of the music. Artists and composers use time signature to communicate these aspects both to the musician and the listener.

\begin{instructorNotes}About the drum machine: 

The website shows a single measure. Each number corresponds to a beat. Each beat then is subdivided into rectangles to allow for shorter notes (eights or sixteenths, depending on the time signature).

Next, each height on the staff corresponds to a different drum. They can choose any drums they want, but all notes on the same line of the staff should be marked on the same drum.

Example solutions are shown for the four passages that students will encode on the drum machine.

In all these examples, the idea is that the upper row marks the rhythm, while the bottom row has two other drums, which play at some of the beats, but not all.
\end{instructorNotes}

\begin{instructorNotes}
Students will work through this question individually on in small groups. 

This question can look a little daunting at first, students might wonder how to decide what "reasonable" is. However, it is not as hard as it looks. Encourage students to start by finding patterns in the note sequence. In the first example, all measures turn out to be equal, so finding the repetition patterns actually solves the problem. After that, remind students to figure out what the time signature is, by adding up the notes on each measure.

In the other questions, the measures have two different forms. For (c), following the same process as before, students are likely to conclude on three measures and a 12/8 time signature. While this isn't incorrect, there's another alternative. Ask students if the current measures can be subdivided and still make sense. They should find that the 12/8 measures can be split "in half" to give a 6/8 time signature.

\end{instructorNotes}
